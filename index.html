Audre Lorde

American writer, feminist, womanist, librarian, and civil rights activist.

Here's a timeline of Audre Lorde's Life:

  • 1934 - Born in New York City
  • 1959 - Graduated from Hunter College with a degree in literature and philosophy
  • 1961 - Earned a master's degree in library science from Columbia, and then worked as a librarian.
  • 1962 - She married Edward Rollins, a legal aid lawyer; they later had two children, Elizabeth and jonathan.
  • 1968 - Lorde published her first collection of poetry, The First Cities, and became poet-in-residence at Tougaloo College, Mississippi
  • 1977 - She gave a speech at the Modern Languages Association conference in Chicago that would become ‘The Transformation of Silence into Language and Action’.
  • 1978 - She was diagnosed with breast cancer and underwent a mastectomy.
  • 1982 - Published her autobiographical novel, Zami: A New Spelling of My Name, and two years later her essays and speeches were collected as Sister Outsider.
  • 1984 - Began teaching at the Freie Universität Berlin.
  • 1987 - Became the first woman to be named Thomas Hunter Professor at Hunter College.
  • 1991 - She was named New York State Poet Laureate
  • 1984 - Died of cancer, aged 58, on 17 November in St Croix.

‘What I love about Audre Lorde is her political and emotional honesty, her passion for living life as herself, her understanding of what a privilege and joy this is. I love her patience, as she taught generations (by now) of women and men the sweet if dangerous fun of self-love.' - Alice Walker
